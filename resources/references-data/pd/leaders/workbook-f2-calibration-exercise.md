**Your name:** ______________________   **School:** ______________________   **Date:** ____________

This workbook goes with the **Observer Calibration** session. You will read short lesson descriptions ("vignettes"), score them against the real F2 rubric, and then compare with your group.

> **The rule for today:** score the **evidence in the lesson**, not the teacher you imagine. Beside every score, write **one line** saying what in the lesson matched the rubric.

## Part A — The rubric you will use

These are the **exact** F2 rubric lines from the Eduversal Teacher Appraisal v2.1 (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → F2`). Read each one. Underline the key word in each level.

### Item D1.1 — Lesson Plan Structure: Layout, Sequencing, and Timing
*(anchors to CTS 3.2 — plan coherent, engaging lessons)*

| Score | What it means |
|---|---|
| **1** | Lesson plan lacks coherent structure. Sequencing is illogical or timing is so unrealistic that it undermines instructional effectiveness. |
| **2** | Lesson plan structure is adequate but shows inconsistencies in sequencing or unrealistic time allocations for some phases. |
| **3** | Lesson plan is well-structured with clear sequencing and reasonable timing. Minor pacing gaps are present but do not undermine coherence. |
| **4** | Lesson plan reflects a carefully structured sequence with logical progression and realistic time allocation for each phase. **Transitions between phases are explicitly planned.** |

### Item D1.2 — Clarity and Measurability of Learning Objectives
*(anchors to CTS 3.2)*

| Score | What it means |
|---|---|
| **1** | Learning objectives are absent, incoherent, or so broad they provide no meaningful guidance for instruction. |
| **2** | Learning objectives are present but vague, unmeasurable, or focused on teacher activity rather than student learning. |
| **3** | Learning objectives are clear and largely measurable. Minor ambiguities in scope or success criteria are present. |
| **4** | Learning objectives are clearly articulated, student-centred, and measurable. They specify what students will know or be able to do, at what level of complexity, and how success will be evidenced. |

### Item D3.2 — Establishing a Productive Class Opening
*(Instructional Process)*

| Score | What it means |
|---|---|
| **1** | The lesson begins without a deliberate opening. Students are not oriented to the learning purpose or brought into productive focus. |
| **2** | The lesson opening is functional but routine. It does little to activate prior knowledge or generate curiosity about the lesson. |
| **3** | The lesson opening is adequate and connects to the learning to come. Students are brought to focus effectively, though the opening could be more intellectually stimulating. |
| **4** | The lesson opening is purposeful and immediately engaging. It activates prior knowledge, establishes relevance, and focuses students on the learning ahead. |

**Year 8 Science — "Forces and Motion" — observed by you, 38 minutes.**

Bu Ratih's written plan lists five phases with times (5 / 10 / 12 / 8 / 3 minutes). The plan notes, in writing, how she will move from the demonstration to group work: *"hand out worksheets while the video loads; signal the move with the bell."* The objective on the plan reads: *"By the end of the lesson, students will be able to calculate speed using distance ÷ time, and explain one real example."*

The lesson begins with a 30-second video of a sprinter. Bu Ratih asks: *"Last week we measured distance. What do we add today to find speed?"* Three students answer; she connects their answers to today's goal within the first two minutes. The five phases run close to the planned times; the move to group work is smooth because worksheets are already out. The lesson ends with a clear closing question, on time.

## Part E — The three scoring traps (tick the ones you caught yourself in)

- ☐ **The halo** — I gave a high score because I like / respect the teacher, not because the lesson showed it.
- ☐ **Scoring intent** — I scored what the teacher *meant* to do, not what *happened*.
- ☐ **Loose rubric reading** — I treated a level-3 phrase ("minor gaps") as if it meant level-4 ("explicitly planned").

### Facilitator answer notes (do not show participants until after they score)

> These are *guidance* scores to anchor the discussion, not the "only right answer." The aim is shared reasoning, not a perfect match.

**Vignette 1 (clear, high):**
- **D1.1 → 4.** Five phases, realistic times, and **transitions are explicitly planned in writing** (the worksheet-and-bell note). The level-4 phrase is met.
- **D1.2 → 4.** Objective is student-centred, measurable, and names the standard ("calculate speed using distance ÷ time, explain one example").
- **D3.2 → 4.** Opening activates prior knowledge ("last week we measured distance") and connects to the goal within two minutes.

**Vignette 2 (borderline, mid):**
- **D1.1 → 2.** Activities are sensible but **times are not allocated**, and two phases over-run so the final task is cut. That is "inconsistencies in sequencing or unrealistic time allocations" → level 2 (not 3, because the over-run undermined the close).
- **D1.2 → 2.** "Students will learn about persuasive techniques" is teacher-activity language, vague, not measurable → level 2.
- **D3.2 → 2.** "Open your books… today we are doing persuasive writing" is functional but routine; it does not activate prior knowledge or build curiosity → level 2.

*If your group landed within one point of these on most items, you are calibrated. Where you differed by two, that is the conversation worth having — go back to the rubric word.*
