# Facilitator Session Guide — Observer Calibration for F2 Class Observation

> **Read `_facilitator-conventions.md` first.** This guide assumes the shared timing blocks, the online/face-to-face variants, and the Glow/Grow/Go stance.

---

## Why this session exists

F2 (Class Observation) is **50% of every teacher's appraisal score** (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → weights.F2 = 0.5`). It is scored by school leaders — principals, coordinators, subject leaders — watching a single 35–40 minute lesson, across **45 items in four domains** (Lesson Planning 10, Classroom Management 8, Instructional Process 21, Assessment 6).

Because a *person* scores it, two honest observers can watch the same lesson and give different scores. If one school's "3" is another school's "4", the appraisal is not fair across the network. **Calibration** fixes this: it trains observers to read the rubric the same way, so the score reflects the *teaching*, not the *observer*.

This is the single most important PD session for leaders, because it protects the integrity of the half of the appraisal that depends on human judgement.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, each participant can:

1. **Explain** what each of the four F2 domains measures and why F2 carries the heaviest weight.
2. **Score** a lesson against the F2 rubric using evidence, not impression.
3. **Justify** a score by quoting the rubric level and the lesson evidence that matches it.
4. **Reconcile** a different score with a colleague by returning to the rubric — not by splitting the difference.

---

## Materials & setup

**You need:**
- The companion workbook `workbook-f2-calibration-exercise.md` — one per participant (printed for face-to-face; link shared for online).
- The "Three Rating Systems at a Glance" one-pager (`../00-program-overview/three-rating-systems-at-a-glance.md`) — in case anyone is unclear where F2 sits.
- **Two short lesson videos OR two written lesson vignettes** (the workbook provides written vignettes you can use if no video is available). One should be a clear case; one should be a genuinely borderline case.
- The F2 rubric extract (the workbook reproduces the exact rubric lines for the items used).

**Face-to-face:** tables of 4–5; printed workbooks; one scoring sheet each.
**Online:** breakout rooms of 3–4; workbook open on screen; a shared poll tool (Mentimeter / Google Form / Zoom poll) to reveal everyone's scores at once.

> **Before the session:** decide which 3 F2 items you will calibrate on. Do not try all 45. Three items, scored well, teaches the method better than 45 scored in a rush. The workbook uses **D1.1 (Lesson Plan Structure)**, **D3 Instructional Process**, and **D4 Assessment** as the worked set — change them if your group's weak spot is elsewhere.

## Facilitator talking points

**On why F2 is 50%.** *"Of everything in the appraisal, this is the half that watches you actually teach. Administration and inspection matter, but a lesson is where teaching lives. That is why F2 is half the score — and why we have to get the scoring right."* (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → weights`).

**On evidence vs impression.** This is the heart of the session. *"The rubric does not ask 'is this a good teacher?' It asks 'what happened in this lesson?' We score what we saw and heard — not the teacher's reputation, not how much we like them, not how hard they tried."*

**On reading the rubric closely.** Use the real D1.1 rubric (Lesson Plan Structure) to show how one word separates a 3 from a 4:
- **Level 3:** *"Lesson plan is well-structured with clear sequencing and reasonable timing. Minor pacing gaps are present but do not undermine coherence."*
- **Level 4:** *"Lesson plan reflects a carefully structured sequence with logical progression and realistic time allocation for each phase. **Transitions between phases are explicitly planned.**"*

*"The difference is not 'better' — it is a specific thing: are transitions* ***explicitly planned****? If yes, and the rest holds, it is a 4. If there are minor pacing gaps and transitions are not planned, it is a 3. We do not guess — we look for the named evidence."* (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → F2 D1.1`; the item anchors to **CTS 3.2 — cambridge/teacher-standards-2023.json**: *"plan coherent, authentic and engaging learning programmes and lessons"*).

**On the three common scoring traps** (name these in the debrief):
1. **The halo.** A teacher we rate highly gets high scores everywhere, even on items where the lesson was weak. Fix: score one item at a time, evidence only.
2. **Scoring intent, not evidence.** "She *meant* to check understanding." The rubric scores what happened, not what was planned.
3. **Reading the rubric loosely.** Treating "minor pacing gaps" (a level 3 phrase) as if it means "perfect" (a level 4). Fix: underline the exact words.

**On reconciling — the key habit.** *"When two of us score the same lesson 3 and 4, we do not average to 3.5. We go back to the rubric. One of us is reading a word more loosely than the other. We find which word, and we agree what it means. That is calibration."*

## Activity instructions

### Activity 1 — Score alone (silent, 24 min)
Each participant scores the **same** vignette on the **same 3 items**, alone, in the workbook. Beside each score they write **one line of evidence** ("students could not say the lesson goal when asked → D1.2 objective not clear to students → level 2"). No discussion. Silence matters — it stops people anchoring on each other.

- **Face-to-face:** paper workbook, pen.
- **Online:** workbook on screen; submit scores via the poll so the reveal is simultaneous.

### Activity 2 — Reconcile (22 min)
Reveal the spread. For every item where the group did **not** agree:
1. Each person reads the rubric level they chose, out loud.
2. The group finds the **one word or phrase** they read differently.
3. They agree what the evidence shows, and re-score.
4. They record: agreed score + the evidence + the rubric phrase that decided it.

- **Online:** one breakout room per group of 3–4; facilitator drops into each. Give the room a single instruction: *"For each disagreement, find the word you read differently."*

## Common questions (answer in simple English)

**"What if the lesson was good but does not match the rubric word?"**
Score the rubric word. If a lesson is genuinely strong in a way the rubric does not capture, note it in the written feedback — but the *number* follows the rubric. That is what keeps it fair across schools.

**"The teacher was nervous because I was watching. Isn't that unfair?"**
We score the lesson we saw. We can be kind about it in the conversation (Glow / Grow / Go), and observation gets less stressful with practice. But the score reflects the evidence.

**"Two of us still disagree after talking. What then?"**
Then the rubric is genuinely ambiguous for this case — note it, score on the side the evidence leans, and flag the item to Eduversal so the rubric can be sharpened. Disagreement that survives the rubric is useful data, not failure.

**"Do teachers see these scores?"**
Yes. F2 is scored live during the observation and feeds the teacher's appraisal. The feedback conversation uses Glow / Grow / Go (see `_facilitator-conventions.md` §6).

## Exit ticket

On paper or a Google Form:
1. One scoring **trap** I now watch for in myself.
2. One thing I will **do** differently in my next observation.
3. One F2 item I am still **unsure** how to score.

> Collect line 3 across the group. The items people are unsure about tell Eduversal where to sharpen the rubric or run a follow-up. This is also the network's evidence that calibration happened.
