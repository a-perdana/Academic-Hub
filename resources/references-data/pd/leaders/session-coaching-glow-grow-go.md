# Facilitator Session Guide — Coaching Your Teachers (Glow / Grow / Go)

> **Read `_facilitator-conventions.md` first** — especially §1 (ESL / EAL), §2 (timing), and §6 (the coaching stance). This session puts §6 into practice.

---

## Why this session exists

After an observation, the *score* is not what changes a teacher's practice — the *conversation* is. But many feedback conversations go wrong in the same ways: too much praise and no clear next step, a long list that overwhelms, or feedback so vague the teacher does not know what to do on Monday.

Eduversal's appraisal already builds in a feedback protocol — **Glow / Grow / Go** — and every scored item carries a `grow` and `go` prompt (`appraisal-framework-v2.json → feedback_prompts`). This session trains leaders to use it well, and to ask **GROW** coaching questions so the teacher does the thinking, not just receives instructions.

This pairs with the **F2 calibration** session: calibration makes the *score* fair; this session makes the *conversation* useful.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of this session, each leader can:

1. **Give** feedback in the Glow / Grow / Go shape — one strength, one area, one next step.
2. **Tie** every point to specific evidence from the lesson, not impressions.
3. **Use** GROW questions so the teacher reaches the next step themselves.
4. **Avoid** the common feedback traps (praise sandwich, long lists, vague advice).

---

## Materials & setup

- The one-pager `onepager-leadership-evaluation-map.md`.
- A "feedback shape" card: Glow / Grow / Go + the GROW questions.
- Two short lesson vignettes (you can reuse the ones in `workbook-f2-calibration-exercise.md`) to practise giving feedback on.

**Face-to-face:** pairs for role-play; printed cards.
**Online:** breakout rooms of 2–3; cards as a shared slide.

## Facilitator talking points

**On the shape: Glow / Grow / Go.** *(`appraisal-framework-v2.json` adds `feedback_prompts.grow` + `.go` to every scored item, plus `follow_up_indicators`.)*
- **Glow** — one specific strength, with evidence. *"When you asked Dina to explain her method, three other students changed their answers — that is checking for understanding in action."* Not "great lesson!"
- **Grow** — one specific area, with evidence. *"In the last ten minutes, six students were not working — the task may have finished too early."* Not a list of five.
- **Go** — one concrete next step, with a date. *"Before next week, plan one extension task for early finishers. Bring it to our chat."*

**On why one-of-each beats a long list.** *"Kraft, Blazar and Hogan studied teacher coaching across many schools. Coaching changes practice when it is specific and focused — not when it is general praise or a long checklist. One Glow, one Grow, one Go. The teacher can actually act on one thing."* (Kraft, Blazar & Hogan, 2018).

**On GROW questions (the coaching move).** Instead of telling, ask:
- **Goal:** *"What do you want this group to be able to do by the end of next week?"*
- **Reality:** *"What did you see today — what is working, what is not?"*
- **Options:** *"What could you try? What else?"*
- **Will:** *"What will you commit to before our next chat?"*

*"When the teacher names the next step, they own it. When you hand it down, they comply. Coaching is asking the right question, not giving the right answer."* (GROW model — `principal-coaching-framework-v1.json → session_structure.agenda_stages.3_focus_topic`).

**On the traps:**
1. **The praise sandwich.** Strength → criticism → strength. Teachers learn to wait for the "but" and ignore the praise. Be honest and clear instead.
2. **The long list.** Five Grows = zero change. Pick the one that matters most.
3. **Vague advice.** "Improve your questioning" is not actionable. "Plan three questions before the lesson, one for each ability group" is.

## Activity instructions

### Role-play the feedback conversation (28 min)
In pairs, use a lesson vignette. One plays the leader, one plays the teacher.
1. The leader gives **one Glow, one Grow, one Go** — each tied to evidence in the vignette.
2. The leader uses at least **one GROW question** so the teacher names the next step.
3. Swap roles with a new vignette.
4. Each gives the other feedback on *how the feedback felt*.

- **Online:** breakout rooms of 2; facilitator drops in and listens for "evidence-based" + "one of each".

> **Why this works:** giving good feedback is a skill that improves with practice, not with theory. Leaders feel, from the teacher's seat, the difference between feedback that lands and feedback that overwhelms.

---

## Common questions (answer in simple English)

**"What if the lesson had many problems?"**
Pick the **one** that matters most for student learning. Fixing one thing well beats trying to fix five and changing nothing. The others can come in later conversations.

**"Isn't only one strength too little?"**
One *specific, evidenced* strength is worth more than five vague ones. The teacher remembers it and repeats it. That is the point.

**"What if the teacher gets defensive?"**
Tie everything to evidence, not opinion ("six students were off-task" is hard to argue with; "your management is weak" invites defence). And ask questions — defensiveness drops when the teacher is thinking, not being judged.

**"How is this different from the F2 score?"**
The score (F2) says *where the teaching is*. The conversation (this session) decides *what changes next*. Calibration makes the score fair; coaching makes the score useful.

---

## Exit ticket

1. One change I will make to how I give feedback.
2. One GROW question I will use in my next conversation.
3. One thing I am still unsure about.

> Collect line 3 — feedback skill is uneven across leaders; the shared uncertainties show you what to coach the coaches on.
